# Introduction

The Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) is transforming modern
manufacturing, industrial automation, smart transportation, and
cyber--physical production systems by interconnecting large numbers of
sensors, controllers, and intelligent devices. These systems
continuously generate latency-sensitive data that must be processed in
near real time to support predictive maintenance, anomaly detection,
process optimization, and autonomous control. Although cloud computing
provides abundant computational resources, transmitting all data to
remote datacenters often introduces excessive latency and network
congestion. Consequently, fog computing has emerged as an effective
paradigm that extends computation and storage resources closer to end
devices, enabling low-latency processing for time-critical industrial
applications [@ref1; @ref2; @ref11].

Efficient workload orchestration remains a fundamental challenge in
large-scale fog-assisted IIoT environments. Existing studies have
proposed various resource-aware, network-aware, and fault-tolerant
scheduling mechanisms to improve workload distribution and resource
utilization [@ref3; @ref6; @ref8; @ref9; @ref10; @ref12; @ref13].
However, most practical deployments operate under highly dynamic
conditions characterized by fluctuating workloads, heterogeneous
resources, varying network conditions, and unexpected node failures.

The first challenge is the reliance on centralized scheduling
coordinators. Many fog architectures employ a master controller or
scheduling node responsible for collecting system information and
allocating workloads. Although effective under normal operation,
coordinator failures can immediately disrupt workload assignment and
create a single point of failure. Existing fault-aware scheduling
schemes generally focus on task execution reliability and provide
limited support for rapid coordinator recovery [@ref12; @ref20].

The second challenge is maintaining accurate and stable scheduling
decisions in dynamic fog environments. Resource availability, queue
occupancy, communication latency, and workload intensity continuously
change over time. Scheduling based solely on instantaneous resource
information can result in workload oscillation, resource imbalance, and
degraded performance under rapidly changing operating conditions
[@ref3; @ref13].

The third challenge concerns deadline-sensitive workloads generated by
industrial monitoring and control systems. Existing scheduling
approaches typically allocate workloads once and provide limited support
for dynamically acquiring additional computational resources after
deployment. Consequently, critical workloads may miss their deadlines
even when spare resources remain available elsewhere in the fog
infrastructure [@ref8; @ref17].

To address these challenges, this paper proposes a stability-aware
load-balancing framework with autonomous leadership recovery for
resilient fog-assisted IIoT systems. The framework introduces a
hierarchical coordination architecture consisting of a Master Fog Node
(MFN) and a Secondary Master Fog Node (SMFN), a Failure Resilience Index
(FRI) for recovery-aware scheduling, and a Recovery Score (RS) mechanism
for rapid coordinator replacement. Furthermore, a recovery-preserving
collaborative resource-assistance mechanism dynamically recruits helper
nodes for overloaded workloads while preserving recovery-critical
resources. To secure system operation, the framework integrates
PUF-based device authentication, CRYSTALS-Kyber key establishment, and
CP-ABE-based result protection.

The major contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

1.  **Stability-Aware and Recovery-Aware Load Balancing:** We design a
    hierarchical MFN--SMFN scheduling architecture that jointly
    considers resource availability, communication latency, workload
    conditions, runtime stability, and failure resilience. The proposed
    Failure Resilience Index (FRI) enables recovery-aware scheduling
    decisions that improve workload distribution while reducing
    coordinator instability.

2.  **Autonomous Leadership Recovery:** We develop a decentralized
    recovery mechanism that continuously replicates scheduling-state
    information across selected fog nodes. Combined with the proposed
    Recovery Score (RS), the framework enables rapid coordinator
    replacement without reconstructing global scheduling information,
    thereby minimizing service disruption during coordinator failures.

3.  **Recovery-Preserving Collaborative Resource Assistance:** We
    propose a collaborative assistance mechanism that dynamically
    recruits helper nodes for overloaded and deadline-sensitive
    workloads while preserving recovery-capable resources. The proposed
    approach improves deadline satisfaction without compromising future
    recovery readiness.

4.  **Integrated Security and Performance Evaluation:** We integrate
    PUF-based device authentication, post-quantum key establishment, and
    CP-ABE-based access control into the proposed framework.
    Experimental results demonstrate superior workload latency, recovery
    responsiveness, deadline satisfaction, and operational resilience
    compared with representative resource-aware, network-aware, and
    fault-tolerant fog scheduling schemes.

# Related Work

## Load Balancing and Task Scheduling in Fog Computing

Efficient workload distribution is a fundamental requirement for
fog-assisted IIoT systems. Existing studies have proposed a variety of
load-balancing and task-scheduling mechanisms to improve resource
utilization, reduce latency, and optimize quality-of-service
performance. Kashani and Mahdipour [@ref2] presented a comprehensive
study of load-balancing algorithms in fog environments and highlighted
the importance of adaptive resource allocation under heterogeneous
computing conditions. Aldossary *et al.* [@ref1] surveyed recent
fog-computing load-balancing techniques and identified scalability,
dynamic resource management, and fault tolerance as major open
challenges.

Several studies have focused on workload-aware and resource-aware
scheduling. Ala'anzy *et al.* [@ref3] proposed a dynamic load-balancing
framework for healthcare-oriented fog environments, while Jasim and
Al-Raweshidy [@ref6] developed an SDN-assisted load-balancing mechanism
for real-time healthcare systems. Yu *et al.* [@ref8] introduced a
location-aware scheduling framework that jointly considers fog and cloud
resources, whereas Vijarania *et al.* [@ref9] investigated
energy-efficient workload allocation in integrated IoT--fog--cloud
infrastructures. Other representative approaches include multi-objective
optimization-based scheduling [@ref10], smart-factory-oriented
energy-aware load balancing [@ref11], task-migration-based scheduling
[@ref12], and distributed learning-based resource allocation [@ref13].

Although these approaches improve workload distribution and resource
utilization, they primarily focus on scheduling optimization under
normal operating conditions. Most assume the continuous availability of
a centralized scheduler or coordinator and provide limited support for
rapid recovery when scheduling entities fail. Furthermore, adaptive
resource assistance for deadline-sensitive workloads is rarely
considered after workload deployment.

## Fault-Tolerant Scheduling and Resilient Edge Computing

To improve service availability in distributed environments, recent
studies have investigated fault-tolerant scheduling and resilient
edge-computing architectures. Xu *et al.* [@ref15] proposed a
fault-tolerant stream-processing framework for serverless edge
environments that accommodates uncertain data rates. Cao *et al.*
[@ref16] developed a preference-aware fault-tolerant function-embedding
mechanism for energy-harvesting edge systems. Taghinezhad-Niar and
Taheri [@ref17] introduced a cost-efficient fault-tolerant scheduling
framework for deadline-constrained IoT workflows across edge--cloud
infrastructures.

Several studies have focused on improving reliability through
redundancy, coding, and intelligent task management. EdgeHydra [@ref18]
employs erasure coding to provide fault-tolerant edge data distribution,
while Long *et al.* [@ref19] proposed reinforcement-learning-based
fault-aware task offloading for mobile edge computing. Chen *et al.*
[@ref20] presented a hybrid fault-tolerance-aware load-balancing
mechanism that jointly considers workload distribution and reliability.
More recently, Lu *et al.* [@ref21] proposed a gossip-enhanced routing
and fault-recovery framework for distributed networks.

Despite these advances, most existing solutions focus on task-level
fault tolerance, redundancy management, or fault-aware scheduling.
Coordinator survivability and autonomous recovery of scheduling services
remain largely unexplored. In many existing systems, coordinator
failures still require centralized recovery procedures, leader
re-election, or external management services, which may introduce
additional recovery delays and service disruption.

# Our Proposed System

This section presents the proposed stability-aware load-balancing
framework with autonomous leadership recovery for resilient fog-assisted
IIoT systems. The framework integrates secure data acquisition, adaptive
workload orchestration, decentralized coordinator recovery, and
protected result outsourcing to ensure low-latency processing,
continuous service availability, and secure data management in dynamic
industrial environments.

### System Entities

The proposed framework consists of the following entities:

1.  **Attribute Authority (AA):** A trusted authority responsible for
    system initialization, generation of cryptographic parameters,
    issuance of attribute-based secret keys, and management of
    access-control policies for authorized users.

2.  **Authorized Users (AUs):** Legitimate users who submit workload
    requests and retrieve processing results. Access to outsourced
    results is granted only when user attributes satisfy the associated
    access-control policies.

3.  **IIoT Devices:** Resource-constrained sensors, actuators, and
    industrial controllers equipped with Physical Unclonable Functions
    (PUFs). Devices generate sensing data and perform lightweight
    authentication and authenticated encryption before transmission.

4.  **CRP Database Server:** A trusted repository that maintains
    challenge--response pairs (CRPs) for registered IIoT devices and
    supports PUF-based device authentication.

5.  **Edge Gateway:** The entry point of the fog infrastructure that
    authenticates incoming device traffic, filters invalid packets,
    performs integrity verification, and aggregates validated data into
    workload batches.

6.  **Master Fog Node (MFN):** The primary scheduling coordinator
    responsible for workload allocation, helper-node selection, and
    orchestration of adaptive load-balancing operations across the fog
    infrastructure.

7.  **Secondary Master Fog Node (SMFN):** A backup coordination node
    that continuously collects runtime telemetry and maintains the
    global fog-node score table. The SMFN periodically replicates
    scheduling-state information to selected fog nodes to facilitate
    autonomous leadership recovery.

8.  **Fog Nodes (FNs):** Distributed computing servers that execute
    assigned workloads, report runtime status, and participate in
    collaborative processing and recovery operations when required.

9.  **Cloud Storage:** An honest-but-curious storage provider that
    stores encrypted workload outputs, metadata, and system logs. Since
    all outsourced data remain encrypted, the cloud cannot access
    plaintext information.

### **Phase I: System Initialization** {#phase-i-system-initialization .unnumbered}

This phase establishes the security, communication, and scheduling
infrastructure of the proposed framework.

#### Step 1: Security Initialization {#step-1-security-initialization .unnumbered}

The Attribute Authority (AA) initializes the CP-ABE system by generating
the public parameters and master secret key $$\begin{equation}
(MPK,MSK)
\leftarrow
\mathsf{Setup}(1^{\lambda},\Omega)
\end{equation}$$

where $\Omega$ denotes the attribute universe. Each authorized user $u$
receives an attribute-bound secret key $$\begin{equation}
SK_u
\leftarrow
\mathsf{KeyGen}(MSK,Attr_u)
\end{equation}$$

For device authentication, the CRP server generates challenge--response
pairs $$\begin{equation}
R_i=f_{\mathrm{PUF}}(C_i)
\end{equation}$$ and stores $$\begin{equation}
CRP_i=(ID_i,C_i,R_i)
\end{equation}$$ for each registered IIoT device.

#### Step 2: Post-Quantum Communication Initialization {#step-2-post-quantum-communication-initialization .unnumbered}

Each gateway and fog node generates a CRYSTALS-Kyber key pair
$$\begin{equation}
(pk_x,sk_x)
\leftarrow
\mathsf{KeyGen}_{\mathrm{Kyber}}(1^{\lambda})
\end{equation}$$ where $x\in{\mathrm{GW},F_1,\ldots,F_n}$. During
registration, each IIoT device establishes a shared root key
$$\begin{equation}
(CT_i^{\mathrm{KEM}},K_i^{\mathrm{root}})
\leftarrow
\mathsf{Encaps}(pk_{\mathrm{GW}})
\end{equation}$$

#### Step 3: Scheduling Infrastructure Initialization {#step-3-scheduling-infrastructure-initialization .unnumbered}

Each fog node continuously monitors its runtime state $$\begin{equation}
\Psi(F_j)
=
(Q_j,C_j,M_j,L_j,U_j)
\end{equation}$$ where $Q_j$, $C_j$, $M_j$, $L_j$, and $U_j$ denote
queue occupancy, computational capability, available memory,
communication latency, and trust score, respectively. Initially,
$$\begin{equation}
U_j^{(0)}=1
\end{equation}$$

### **Phase II: Hardware-Bound IIoT Protection** {#phase-ii-hardware-bound-iiot-protection .unnumbered}

This phase protects sensed data before it enters the fog infrastructure
by combining PUF-based device identity with lightweight authenticated
encryption.

#### Step 1: Hardware-Bound Key Derivation {#step-1-hardware-bound-key-derivation .unnumbered}

Using the challenge--response pair established in Phase I, device $D_i$
reconstructs its stable PUF secret $$\begin{equation}
R_{secret,i}
\leftarrow
\mathsf{FuzzyRep}
(
R_{noisy,i},
HD_i
)
\end{equation}$$

The device then derives a hardware-bound root key $$\begin{equation}
K_{base,i}
=
\mathsf{KDF}
\left(
K_{root,i}
\parallel
R_{secret,i}
\parallel
ID_i
\right)
\end{equation}$$ where $K_{root,i}$ denotes the post-quantum shared
secret established during Phase I.

#### Step 2: Secure Packet Protection {#step-2-secure-packet-protection .unnumbered}

For each sensed data item $m_i$, the device derives a fresh packet key
$$\begin{equation}
K_{p,i}
=
\mathsf{KDF}
(
K_{base,i}
\parallel
t_i
\parallel
ctr_i
)
\end{equation}$$ where $t_i$ and $ctr_i$ denote the packet timestamp and
counter.

The associated authenticated data is $$\begin{equation}
AAD_i
=
ID_i
\parallel
t_i
\parallel
ctr_i
\end{equation}$$

The device then encrypts and authenticates the packet using
ChaCha20--Poly1305: $$\begin{equation}
(CT_i,Tag_i)
\leftarrow
\mathsf{AEAD.Enc}
(
K_{p,i},
N_i,
m_i,
AAD_i
)
\end{equation}$$

#### Step 3: Packet Generation {#step-3-packet-generation .unnumbered}

The protected packet is constructed as $$\begin{equation}
P_i
=
{
ID_i,
CT_i,
N_i,
Tag_i,
AAD_i
}
\end{equation}$$ and transmitted to the gateway for validation, workload
formation, and scheduling in Phase III.

### **Phase III: Scheduling-Aware Workload Profiling and Batch Formation** {#phase-iii-scheduling-aware-workload-profiling-and-batch-formation .unnumbered}

This phase performs packet validation, replay filtering, and workload
profiling before workload submission to the fog layer. By removing
invalid traffic and constructing scheduling-aware workload descriptors
at the gateway, the framework reduces unnecessary processing overhead
and improves subsequent load-balancing decisions.

#### Step 1: Packet Validation {#step-1-packet-validation .unnumbered}

For each received packet $$\begin{equation}
P_i
=
{
ID_i,
CT_i,
N_i,
Tag_i,
AAD_i
}
\end{equation}$$ the gateway verifies the authenticated-encryption tag
and freshness information contained in $(t_i,ctr_i)$. A packet is
accepted only if integrity verification succeeds and no replay condition
is detected. Invalid packets are discarded immediately.

#### Step 2: Micro-Batch Formation {#step-2-micro-batch-formation .unnumbered}

All validated packets received within aggregation window $t_{window}$
are grouped into $$\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_{valid}
=
{P_i \mid \mathsf{Verify}(P_i)=1}
\end{equation}$$

To provide batch-level integrity, the gateway computes a commitment
$$\begin{equation}
GTag
=
\mathcal{H}
\Big(
\mathsf{Sort}
(
{h_i}
)
\parallel
t_G
\Big)
\end{equation}$$ where $$\begin{equation}
h_i
=
\mathcal{H}
(
ID_i
\parallel
CT_i
\parallel
Tag_i
)
\end{equation}$$ and $t_G$ denotes the gateway timestamp.

The resulting batch is $$\begin{equation}
B_k
=
(
BID,
\mathcal{P}_{valid},
GTag,
t_G
)
\end{equation}$$

#### Step 3: Scheduling-Aware Workload Profiling {#step-3-scheduling-aware-workload-profiling .unnumbered}

To support adaptive scheduling and recovery operations, the gateway
derives a workload profile $$\begin{equation}
\Phi(B_k)
=
(
S_k,
\omega_k,
\delta_k,
\rho_k
)
\end{equation}$$ where $S_k$ denotes the batch size, $\omega_k$ denotes
workload intensity, $\delta_k$ denotes deadline urgency, and $\rho_k$
denotes recovery priority.

The workload intensity is estimated as $$\begin{equation}
\omega_k
=
\alpha_1 S_k
+
\alpha_2 V_k
+
\alpha_3 D_k
\end{equation}$$ where $V_k$ represents the aggregated payload volume
and $D_k$ denotes the expected processing complexity.

To prioritize critical workloads during overload or recovery conditions,
the gateway computes $$\begin{equation}
\rho_k
=
\beta_1 \delta_k
+
\beta_2 \omega_k
+
\beta_3 P_k
\end{equation}$$ where $P_k$ denotes the application-defined workload
priority.

The resulting pair $$\begin{equation}
(B_k,\Phi(B_k))
\end{equation}$$ is forwarded to the Master Fog Node (MFN). The workload
profile is subsequently used for stability-aware scheduling, helper-node
selection, and autonomous leadership recovery in the following phases.

### **Phase IV: Stability-Aware Load Balancing and Recovery-Oriented Coordination** {#phase-iv-stability-aware-load-balancing-and-recovery-oriented-coordination .unnumbered}

This phase performs workload scheduling while maintaining recovery
readiness throughout the fog infrastructure. Unlike conventional
schedulers that optimize only resource utilization, the proposed
framework jointly considers runtime stability, resource availability,
and failure resilience.

#### Step 1: Stability-Aware Coordinator Election {#step-1-stability-aware-coordinator-election .unnumbered}

Using the telemetry information collected in Phase I, each fog node
$F_j$ computes a coordination score $$\begin{equation}
S^{coord}_j
=
\alpha_1 C_j
+
\alpha_2 M_j
-
\alpha_3 L_j
+
\alpha_4 U_j
+
\alpha_5 R_j
\end{equation}$$ where $C_j$, $M_j$, $L_j$, $U_j$, and $R_j$ denote
computational capability, available memory, communication latency, trust
score, and readiness factor, respectively.

The readiness factor is defined as $$\begin{equation}
R_j
=
1-
(
\beta_1 \bar{Q}_j
+
\beta_2 \bar{M}_j
+
\beta_3 \bar{L}_j
)
\end{equation}$$

To reduce leadership oscillation, a stability penalty is applied:
$$\begin{equation}
\widehat{S}^{coord}_j
=
S^{coord}_j
-
\gamma \Delta S_j
\end{equation}$$ The fog nodes with the highest and second-highest
scores are selected as the Master Fog Node (MFN) and Secondary Master
Fog Node (SMFN), respectively.

#### Step 2: Failure Resilience Assessment {#step-2-failure-resilience-assessment .unnumbered}

To quantify long-term reliability, each fog node computes a Failure
Resilience Index (FRI): $$\begin{equation}
FRI_j
=
\theta_1 U_j
+
\theta_2 R_j
-
\theta_3 FR_j
\end{equation}$$ where $FR_j$ denotes the normalized historical failure
rate. A larger $FRI_j$ indicates stronger operational stability and
recovery capability.

#### Step 3: Recovery-Aware Workload Scheduling {#step-3-recovery-aware-workload-scheduling .unnumbered}

For each workload pair $(B_k,\Phi(B_k))$, the MFN evaluates candidate
fog nodes using $$\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
S^{sched}_{j,k}
=
&\;
w_1 Q_j
+
w_2 L_j
-
w_3 C_j \\
&
-
w_4 M_j
-
w_5 U_j
-
w_6 FRI_j \\
&
+
w_7 \rho_k
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}$$ where $\rho_k$ denotes the recovery-priority score of
workload $B_k$.

To improve scheduling stability, exponential smoothing is applied:
$$\begin{equation}
\widetilde{S}_{j,k}
=
\eta S^{sched}_{j,k}
+
(1-\eta)\widetilde{S}^{prev}_{j,k}
\end{equation}$$

The workload is assigned to $$\begin{equation}
F_k^*
=
\arg\min_{F_j}
\widetilde{S}_{j,k}
\end{equation}$$

#### Step 4: Recovery-State Maintenance {#step-4-recovery-state-maintenance .unnumbered}

To support autonomous leadership recovery, the SMFN periodically
constructs and replicates a scheduling-state snapshot $$\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_{epoch}
=
(Epoch,MFN,SMFN,\mathcal{T})
\end{equation}$$ where $\mathcal{T}$ denotes the latest global telemetry
table.

Each cache node stores the most recent snapshot and computes a Recovery
Score $$\begin{equation}
RS_j
=
\mu_1 \widehat{S}^{coord}_j
+
\mu_2 FRI_j
+
\mu_3 CF_j
\end{equation}$$ where $CF_j$ denotes cache freshness. The replicated
recovery state enables immediate coordinator replacement without
performing a global re-election process. The selected fog node
subsequently executes workload batch $B_k$ and reports runtime updates
to the MFN and SMFN.

### **Phase V: Recovery-Preserving Collaborative Resource Assistance** {#phase-v-recovery-preserving-collaborative-resource-assistance .unnumbered}

This phase enables overloaded or deadline-sensitive workloads to acquire
additional computational resources from neighboring fog nodes while
preserving recovery readiness. Unlike conventional cooperative
scheduling approaches that maximize resource utilization alone, the
proposed framework jointly considers workload urgency and recovery
capability.

#### Step 1: Assistance Request Generation {#step-1-assistance-request-generation .unnumbered}

During execution, the hosting fog node continuously estimates the
workload completion time. Assistance is triggered whenever
$$\begin{equation}
T_k^{est}
>
T_k^{deadline}
\end{equation}$$ or when the workload exhibits high urgency score
$\delta_k$ or recovery priority $\rho_k$.

The required computational deficit is estimated as $$\begin{equation}
\Delta C_k
=
\max
\left(
0,
C_k^{req}
-
C_k^{avail}
\right)
\end{equation}$$ and the assistance request $$\begin{equation}
AR_k
=
(B_k,\Delta C_k,\delta_k,\rho_k)
\end{equation}$$ is forwarded to the MFN.

#### Step 2: Recovery-Preserving Helper Selection {#step-2-recovery-preserving-helper-selection .unnumbered}

Using the latest telemetry table $\mathcal{T}$, the MFN evaluates
candidate helper nodes through the recovery capability score
$$\begin{equation}
RC_i
=
\psi_1 FRI_i
+
\psi_2 RS_i
\end{equation}$$ and computes $$\begin{equation}
HScore_i
=
\lambda_1 A_i
+
\lambda_2 RC_i
-
\lambda_3 L_i
\end{equation}$$ where $A_i$ and $L_i$ denote available computational
capacity and communication latency, respectively.

To preserve autonomous recovery capability, nodes satisfying
$$\begin{equation}
RC_i
\ge
\tau_R
\end{equation}$$ are preferentially reserved for future recovery
operations whenever sufficient alternative helpers exist.

#### Step 3: Collaborative Assistance and Aggregation {#step-3-collaborative-assistance-and-aggregation .unnumbered}

For each selected helper node, an assistance budget is allocated as
$$\begin{equation}
AB_i
=
\kappa A_i FRI_i
\end{equation}$$

The remaining workload is partitioned proportionally to the available
assistance budgets: $$\begin{equation}
W_i
=
\frac{AB_i}
{\sum_{F_m \in \mathcal{H}_k} AB_m}
\cdot
\Delta C_k
\end{equation}$$

Helper nodes process their assigned workload fragments in parallel and
return the resulting outputs to the hosting fog node, which reconstructs
the final result as $$\begin{equation}
M_k
=
\mathsf{Combine}
(M_1,M_2,\ldots,M_h)
\end{equation}$$

By jointly optimizing deadline satisfaction and recovery readiness, the
proposed mechanism improves workload responsiveness while preserving
sufficient resources for future coordinator-recovery operations.

### **Phase VI: Autonomous Leadership Recovery and Secure Result Management** {#phase-vi-autonomous-leadership-recovery-and-secure-result-management .unnumbered}

This phase maintains service continuity during coordinator failures and
securely manages workload results after execution. By combining
autonomous recovery with protected result outsourcing, the framework
ensures uninterrupted operation and reliable result delivery.

#### Step 1: Coordinator Failure Detection {#step-1-coordinator-failure-detection .unnumbered}

Each cache node continuously monitors the liveness of the MFN and SMFN
through periodic heartbeat messages.

A coordinator is considered failed if $$\begin{equation}
t_{now}
-
t_{last}
>
\tau_H
\end{equation}$$ where $t_{last}$ denotes the timestamp of the most
recent heartbeat and $\tau_H$ is the failure-detection threshold.

#### Step 2: Autonomous Leadership Recovery {#step-2-autonomous-leadership-recovery .unnumbered}

Upon detecting a coordinator failure, cache nodes utilize the replicated
scheduling state $$\begin{equation}
\mathcal{C}_{epoch}
=
(
Epoch,
MFN,
SMFN,
\mathcal{T}
)
\end{equation}$$ generated in Phase IV.

Instead of performing a global re-election, each cache node computes the
recovery score $$\begin{equation}
RS_j
=
\mu_1 \widehat{S}^{coord}_j
+
\mu_2 FRI_j
+
\mu_3 CF_j
\end{equation}$$ where $CF_j$ denotes cache freshness.

The replacement coordinator is selected as $$\begin{equation}
MFN^{new}
=
\arg\max_{F_j}
RS_j
\end{equation}$$ The promoted node immediately resumes scheduling
operations using the replicated state table, thereby minimizing service
interruption.

#### Step 3: Result Aggregation {#step-3-result-aggregation .unnumbered}

After workload execution or collaborative assistance, the hosting fog
node collects all execution outputs $$\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_k
=
{
R_1,
R_2,
\ldots,
R_h
}
\end{equation}$$ and reconstructs the final workload result
$$\begin{equation}
R_k
=
\mathsf{Combine}
(

## Recovery Hierarchy

The proposed framework adopts a hierarchical recovery strategy to
minimize service disruption under coordinator failures.

- **Level 1 Recovery:** If the MFN fails, the SMFN immediately assumes
  the coordinator role using the latest replicated scheduling state.

- **Level 2 Recovery:** If both the MFN and SMFN become unavailable, the
  cache node with the highest Recovery Score $RS_j$ and the most recent
  scheduling-state snapshot is promoted as the new coordinator.

- **Level 3 Recovery:** If no eligible cache node satisfies the
  predefined recovery criteria, a decentralized voting procedure is
  invoked to elect a replacement coordinator.

Throughout the recovery process, worker fog nodes continue executing
previously assigned workloads. Only new scheduling decisions are
temporarily suspended until a new coordinator becomes available, thereby
preserving service continuity and preventing workload interruption.

# Security Analysis

We consider an adversary capable of eavesdropping, modifying, replaying,
and injecting messages over the communication network. Fog nodes and
cloud servers may also experience failures or become unavailable. The
Attribute Authority (AA), CRP database server, and underlying
cryptographic primitives are assumed to be trusted.

**Device Authentication and Clone Resistance:** The framework employs
PUF-based authentication to establish hardware-bound device identities.
Communication keys are derived from device-specific PUF secrets and
post-quantum shared secrets, preventing adversaries from cloning
legitimate devices or generating valid authentication credentials
without access to the original hardware.

**Communication Security and Replay Resistance:** Device-to-gateway
communications are protected using CRYSTALS-Kyber key establishment and
ChaCha20--Poly1305 authenticated encryption. Consequently, transmitted
packets remain confidential and any modification of ciphertexts,
timestamps, counters, or authenticated data is detected during
verification. Packet freshness is enforced through timestamp and counter
validation, enabling replayed packets to be identified and discarded.

**Access Control and Data Confidentiality:** Outsourced processing
results are protected through CP-ABE-based access control. The
result-encryption key is encapsulated under an access policy defined by
the AA, ensuring that only authorized users whose attributes satisfy the
policy can recover the protected data. Therefore, unauthorized users and
honest-but-curious cloud servers cannot access plaintext information.

**Resilient Workload Orchestration:** To eliminate single points of
failure, the framework continuously replicates scheduling-state
information across designated cache nodes. Combined with the Failure
Resilience Index (FRI), Recovery Score (RS), and autonomous leadership
recovery mechanism, coordinator replacement can be performed rapidly
without reconstructing global scheduling information. Furthermore,
recovery-preserving collaborative assistance reserves recovery-capable
nodes during overload conditions, maintaining both workload
responsiveness and future recovery readiness.

# Performance Evaluation

This section evaluates the effectiveness of the proposed framework in
terms of load-balancing efficiency, leadership recovery, deadline
satisfaction, and overall system resilience.

## Experimental Setup

The proposed framework was implemented in Python 3.12 and evaluated on a
workstation with an Intel Core i7 processor, 32 GB RAM, and Ubuntu
Linux. The testbed consists of one gateway, eight heterogeneous fog
nodes, and one cloud server. Each fog node periodically reports
telemetry information, including resource availability, queue occupancy,
communication latency, trust score, and failure statistics. To emulate
realistic industrial operating conditions, synthetic IIoT workloads are
generated from 100--1000 virtual devices. Packet sizes range from 256 B
to 8 KB, while workload deadlines vary between 100 ms and 5 s. The
workload arrival rate is progressively increased to create congestion
and overload scenarios. In addition, random MFN and SMFN failures are
injected throughout the experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of the
proposed autonomous recovery mechanism.

The proposed framework is compared with three representative
fog-computing scheduling schemes: (i) the resource-aware load-balancing
approach of Ala'anzy *et al.* [@ref3], (ii) the SDN-assisted scheduling
framework of Jasim and Al-Raweshidy [@ref6], and (iii) the
fault-tolerance-aware load-balancing scheme of Chen *et al.* [@ref20].
All reported results are averaged over 20 independent runs.

## Experiment 1: Workload Completion Latency

This experiment evaluates the effectiveness of the proposed
stability-aware scheduling mechanism under increasing workload
intensity. The workload arrival rate is varied from $10^2$ to $10^4$
workload batches, and the average workload completion latency is
measured.

The proposed framework consistently achieves the lowest workload

scheduling strategy, which jointly considers resource availability,
communication latency, workload conditions, trustworthiness, and failure
resilience through the FRI metric. As workload intensity increases, the
performance advantage becomes increasingly significant because unstable
and overloaded nodes are proactively avoided during scheduling
decisions.

In contrast, the proposed framework continuously maintains replicated
scheduling-state snapshots and precomputed Recovery Scores (RS) across
cache nodes. Upon detecting a coordinator failure, an eligible recovery
node can immediately assume leadership using the latest scheduling state
without reconstructing global system information. As a result, the
proposed framework achieves substantially lower recovery latency and
minimizes service interruption.

By contrast, the proposed framework dynamically recruits helper nodes
based on both computational availability and recovery capability.
Through the combined use of the Failure Resilience Index (FRI) and
Recovery Score (RS), resource assistance is provided without exhausting
recovery-critical nodes. Consequently, the framework improves workload
responsiveness while preserving sufficient recovery readiness for future
failures.

The proposed framework achieves superior resilience by integrating
stability-aware scheduling, autonomous leadership recovery, and
recovery-preserving collaborative assistance into a unified
orchestration framework. Even under simultaneous overload and
coordinator failures, workload execution continues with minimal
disruption, resulting in significantly higher service availability and
workload completion rates.

# Conclusion

This paper presented a stability-aware load-balancing framework with
autonomous leadership recovery for resilient fog-assisted IIoT systems.
The proposed framework integrates a hierarchical MFN--SMFN architecture,
a Failure Resilience Index (FRI) for recovery-aware scheduling, a
Recovery Score (RS) for rapid coordinator replacement, and a
recovery-preserving collaborative assistance mechanism for
deadline-sensitive workloads. In addition, PUF-based authentication,
CRYSTALS-Kyber key establishment, and CP-ABE access control provide
secure system operation. Experimental results demonstrated superior
workload latency, recovery responsiveness, deadline satisfaction, and
overall resilience compared with representative fog-computing scheduling
approaches. Future work will investigate predictive failure-aware
scheduling and large-scale deployment in distributed industrial
environments.
